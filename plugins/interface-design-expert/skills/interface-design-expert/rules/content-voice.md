# Content & Voice — interface-design-expert lens
**Impact: HIGH**

Words are UI. Write for the user's goal. This dimension is qualitative — no platform numbers to
resolve.

## Principles

- **Plain, simple, direct.** Prioritize a goal-oriented style over strict grammar; be concise but
  not robotic.
- **One consistent voice, contextual tone.** *Voice* (the product's mood/attitude/outlook) stays
  constant; *tone* varies by moment — lighter in onboarding, calm and helpful in errors, brief in
  confirmations.
- **Lead with the user's benefit or action.** Label buttons with the verb of what happens
  ("Save changes", not "OK"). Front-load the important word.
- **Error messages help, don't blame.** Say what happened, why, and how to fix it.
- **Be consistent in terminology.** The same concept gets the same word everywhere.
- **Structure content by meaning, not presentation.** Model content by what it *is* (a product, a
  step, a question-and-answer pair), not how it looks, and keep related pieces together as one
  reusable unit so it survives reuse across pages, search results, voice, and future surfaces.
- **Organize by the user's task, not internal structure.** Replace FAQ dumps and feature-led pages
  with purposeful, task-titled content; write short topic headings ("Online payment options")
  instead of repetitive question strings, so the page stays scannable.
- **Keep each fact in one place and reuse it.** Duplicated copy drifts out of sync and erodes
  trust; front-load the user's intent over the brand's framing.
- **Write for linear consumption.** Use device-agnostic verbs ("choose"/"select", not
  "click"/"tap"), order instructions chronologically ("next…", not "the button below"), and put
  critical information *before* the field or action it governs.

## Review — what to look for

*Weight these against the stated intent (see [`../SKILL.md`](../SKILL.md) → Tradeoffs & intent): floor breaches (accessibility, contrast, color-only meaning, focus, safety) are defects regardless of goal; dials are judged fit-or-misfit to the intent; opportunities (optional techniques) are judged "would this help here?" — their absence is not a finding.*

- Is the language plain, direct, and goal-oriented — concise but not robotic?
- Is the voice consistent, with tone that fits the moment (onboarding / error / confirmation)?
- Are buttons labeled with the action verb, important word front-loaded?
- Do error messages say what happened, why, and how to fix it — without blaming the user?
- Is terminology consistent for the same concept throughout?
- Is content structured by meaning (reusable units), not by how it happens to be laid out?
- Is it organized by user task (no FAQ dumps), with scannable topic headings?
- Is each fact stated once and reused, with no drifting duplicates?
- Is it written for linear consumption (device-agnostic verbs, chronological order, critical info before the control)?
